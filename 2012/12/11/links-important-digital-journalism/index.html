Why links are so important to digital journalism

Once upon a time, in a faraway (and frequently nightmarish) land where websites were still a new and mysterious thing, I had strict instructions not to use hyperlinks on my newspaper’s site.

“We don’t want to drive traffic away from our site,” was the reasoning passed down.

That’s crap, and thank goodness journalists have evolved to understand that. As a member of Thunderdome’s curation team, my focus is building digital friendly stories.

While that means a lot of things, linking is an essential part of the package — as well it should be, for any story posted online. Here are some good reasons:

Links provide attribution and boost credibility

• Attribution is just as important now as it always has been. Whether the attribution is owed to source materials, like a study or public documents available online, or to other journalistic works, links are a fantastic step up from what print allows.

“‘According to documents’ or ‘as reported by’ may have been as far as print could go, but that’s not good enough when the sources are online,” wrote Jonathan Stray for the Nieman Journalism Lab.

Even for quoted sources, consider linking to a biographical page or social media account, like Twitter.

• Build trust and credibility. Giving readers the ability to check up on your work boosts credibility, too — and that’s a commodity hard to come by online.

In terms of building trust, leading web usability expert Jakob Nielsen writes that a site should be “Connected to the rest of the Web with links in and out. Not being afraid to link to other sites is a sign of confidence … Isolated sites feel like they have something to hide.”

More reasons to use links

• Readers like links. This Pew Study about online news consumption, though a couple years old, drives home the point with one graphic:

Pew Research Center, 2010

• Links can be a resource for readers. If you’re writing about a concert, why not provide a link to where tickets can be purchased? If it’s a charity, how to make donations. If it’s a restaurant, how to make online reservations. Take your readers there. Do the work for them. It’s a valuable service.

• Foster an active online experience. “People know that most web pages are useless, and they behave accordingly to avoid wasting more time than absolutely necessary on bad pages,” writes Nielsen.

Two things about that:

  • Decrease your story’s uselessness by using links that act as resources for your reader, and;
  • Understand that people never stay anywhere long, so foster that inevitable quickly-moving pace with quality links.

• Be a resource and create context for your stories. A lot of stories have long lives. Consider the average crime story — it starts with breaking coverage of the crime and continues as the case makes it way to court. Every new story should link to previous coverage. The links provide readers with background, leaves a clear trail documenting the story and can drive traffic to your previous coverage.

Even for one-and-done stories, consider linking to related content. If a person is reading about a break-in nearby, he or she might be interested in browsing past stories about other break-ins in that same neighborhood. Link not just because it’s popular with readers, but because it adds value to your story by creating greater context.

• It’s not always easy, but it’s always worthwhile. Depending on the content management system being used, inserting hyperlinks can be super easy or super difficult. Even for those with an easy-to-use CMS, it still takes time to look up all those URLs.

The benefits of using links far outweigh the time spent creating them, however. Plus, it’s the Internet. Everything is connected. Using links is an essential part of being a good steward for the online community.

One thing you should always do with a hyperlink

Links should always open in new windows. Your CMS may offer that as a clickable option after inserting a link or you may have to do it manually with simple HTML code.

If you need the code, here it is: <a href=”URL HERE” target=”_blank”>TEXT HERE</a>

One thing you should never do with a hyperlink

Don’t use “Click here” or “Read more” or any other nondescript term as your hyperlink. Search engines care about the words contained in a link, so make sure the link text is focused on relevant, quality keywords.

For inspiration, The Huffington Post’s regular feature Seven and a half things you need to know today is a consistently great example of well-executed linking.
